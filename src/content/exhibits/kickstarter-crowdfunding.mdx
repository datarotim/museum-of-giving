Kickstarter launched in April 2009 and by 2010 was redefining what it meant to fund something online. Its tagline — "A new way to Fund & Follow Creativity" — captured a genuinely new idea: that ordinary people could collectively finance projects they wanted to see exist, from films and albums to hardware and games. The platform's all-or-nothing funding model, where projects only received money if they hit their goal, created urgency and social momentum. Backers weren't donating — they were pre-ordering a future they helped create.

While Kickstarter is not a charitable platform, its influence on the giving landscape was profound. It trained millions of people to trust online payment flows, to back strangers based on a compelling story, and to share campaigns with their networks. The psychological patterns Kickstarter popularized — progress bars, stretch goals, social proof through backer counts, countdown timers — became standard tools in nonprofit fundraising. By the time Kickstarter had processed over $7 billion in pledges, the behavioral shift was irreversible: people expected to fund things directly, transparently, and socially. Every modern donation page owes something to Kickstarter's proof that crowds will fund what they care about.
