The US Treasury Department launched the War Savings Stamps program in 1917 to fund America's entry into World War I, and this poster specifically targeted children as participants. Stamps cost as little as 25 cents, making them accessible to young people who could save their allowances and small earnings. Children were encouraged to fill stamp books and trade them for War Savings Certificates that matured with interest. Schools organized competitions, and teachers incorporated stamp purchases into civics lessons, turning the classroom into a fundraising channel.

The campaign was remarkably effective and deeply consequential for the future of fundraising. By bringing children into the act of giving, the Treasury Department accomplished two things: it tapped a previously ignored donor segment, and it socialized an entire generation into the habit of contributing to collective causes. The low entry price point — a quarter — democratized participation in a way that large war bond denominations could not. This micro-donation concept would echo through the decades, from March of Dimes coin collectors to modern round-up-your-purchase charity programs. The poster's direct address to "Boys and Girls" also pioneered age-targeted fundraising messaging, a segmentation strategy that nonprofits would not fully rediscover until the late twentieth century.
