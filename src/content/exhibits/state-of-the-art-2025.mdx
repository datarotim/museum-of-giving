The state of fundraising technology in 2025 is defined by convergence: AI-generated appeals personalized to each donor, embedded giving experiences that live inside social platforms and messaging apps, voice-activated donations through smart speakers, and predictive models that identify the right moment to ask. The donation form — the central artifact of online giving for three decades — is dissolving into the background, replaced by contextual giving moments woven into daily digital life.

This exhibit marks not an endpoint but a threshold. The tools of 2025 can predict who will give, how much they'll give, and when to ask — with increasing accuracy. AI can draft personalized appeals, optimize delivery timing, and dynamically adjust ask amounts in real time. But the fundamental challenge remains what it has always been: connecting a person who has resources with a cause that needs them, and making that connection feel meaningful rather than transactional. The technology has changed beyond recognition since the first Red Cross poster. The human impulse it serves has not changed at all. That is perhaps the most important thing the Museum of Giving has to teach us.
