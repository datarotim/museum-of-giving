The Second World War saw the American Red Cross mount its largest-ever fundraising campaigns, combining war bond drives with direct appeals for blood donations, medical supplies, and servicemember support. The posters of this era — bold, graphic, emotionally charged — drew on two decades of refinement in visual persuasion. Norman Rockwell, J. Howard Miller, and other leading illustrators lent their talents to the cause.

What distinguished the WWII campaigns was their sophistication. The Red Cross segmented audiences, tailored messages for factory workers versus housewives versus business owners, and coordinated across print, radio, and in-person canvassing simultaneously. This was arguably the first truly multichannel fundraising operation, and it raised hundreds of millions of dollars. The organizational playbook it produced — campaign planning, donor segmentation, channel coordination — became the foundation of modern nonprofit fundraising practice.
