GivingTuesday launched on November 27, 2012, conceived by New York's 92nd Street Y and the United Nations Foundation as a day of charitable giving following Black Friday and Cyber Monday. The concept was open-source by design: any organization could participate, there was no central platform or fee, and the only requirement was using the hashtag #GivingTuesday. The first year generated $10 million in online donations.

The brilliance of GivingTuesday was its simplicity and its openness. By creating a shared moment — a Schelling point for generosity — rather than a platform or product, it generated network effects that no single organization could have achieved alone. Every nonprofit promoted GivingTuesday because it benefited them directly. Every donor participated because their friends were doing it. The movement grew exponentially: by 2023, GivingTuesday generated an estimated $3.1 billion in US donations in a single day. It demonstrated that the most powerful fundraising innovation might not be technological at all — it might be cultural. Sometimes what giving needs isn't a better form, but a better reason to fill it out today.
