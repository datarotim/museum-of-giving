Near East Relief, originally organized in 1915 as the American Committee for Armenian and Syrian Relief, became one of the most significant humanitarian organizations of the early twentieth century. Its "Lest They Perish" campaign used stark photographic imagery of Armenian genocide survivors — starving children, displaced families, ravaged villages — to compel American donors to contribute to a crisis thousands of miles away. The poster represented a watershed moment: ordinary Americans were being asked to fund relief for people they had never met, in a region most could not find on a map.

The organization raised over $117 million between 1915 and 1930, cared for more than a million refugees, and operated orphanages housing tens of thousands of children. Near East Relief demonstrated that mass media campaigns could mobilize broad public giving for international causes, not just domestic or wartime needs. It was congressionally chartered in 1919 — the first American organization to receive such status for overseas humanitarian work. The fundraising techniques it pioneered — photographic evidence of suffering, specific dollar-to-impact framing, celebrity endorsements — became the template for every international relief campaign that followed, from CARE to UNICEF to modern disaster response appeals.
