Starting in 2010, the Wikimedia Foundation began running annual fundraising banners across Wikipedia, one of the world's most-visited websites. The appeals were disarmingly simple: a photo of founder Jimmy Wales accompanied by a personal message asking readers to donate. No matching gifts, no countdown timers, no social proof widgets — just a direct, slightly uncomfortable request from a real person. The banners appeared to hundreds of millions of visitors worldwide, making them arguably the most-seen fundraising appeals in history.

The results were staggering. Wikipedia's annual fundraising campaigns consistently raised tens of millions of dollars, eventually exceeding $150 million per year, almost entirely from small individual donations. The campaign became a masterclass in conversion at scale: even with single-digit response rates, the sheer volume of impressions generated massive revenue. The Wikimedia Foundation obsessively A/B tested every element — headline copy, photo selection, banner placement, ask amounts — turning their fundraising operation into one of the most data-driven in the world. Wikipedia's approach proved that fundraising doesn't require emotional manipulation or urgency tricks. Sometimes the most effective ask is the most honest one: we built something you use every day, and it costs money to keep running.
