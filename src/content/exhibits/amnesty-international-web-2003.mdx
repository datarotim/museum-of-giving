By 2003, Amnesty International had built a web presence that served a dual purpose rare among charities of the era: it functioned as both an activism platform and a fundraising channel. Visitors could read urgent action appeals about political prisoners, sign petitions, and donate — all in the same session. For an organization built on letter-writing campaigns and grassroots advocacy, the web offered a natural extension of its core model: turning awareness into action.

Amnesty's approach foreshadowed a pattern that would become central to digital fundraising. Organizations that gave supporters something to do beyond giving money — sign a petition, share a story, join a campaign — built deeper engagement and, ultimately, more durable donor relationships. The website demonstrated that the internet wasn't just a new way to collect donations; it was a new way to build movements. The line between activist and donor blurred online in ways it never had in direct mail, and Amnesty was among the first to recognize and exploit that convergence.
