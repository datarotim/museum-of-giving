This May 2000 capture of the Salvation Army's homepage shows "Donate Online" as a top-level navigation item, represented by the organization's iconic red kettle icon. The placement is significant: online giving had graduated from a buried link or afterthought to a primary call-to-action alongside "About Us," "Services," and "Disaster Relief."

For an organization whose fundraising identity was built on the physical presence of bell ringers, elevating "Donate Online" to the main navigation represented a strategic commitment to digital giving. Behind that link lay one of the early challenges of online fundraising: building trust. Donors needed to be convinced that typing a credit card number into a webpage was safe. Early donation pages relied on Verisign SSL certificate seals, padlock icons, and reassuring language about encryption — visual trust signals that served as the digital equivalent of a locked collection box. The infrastructure of online trust that we now take for granted had to be built from scratch, one nervous donor at a time.
