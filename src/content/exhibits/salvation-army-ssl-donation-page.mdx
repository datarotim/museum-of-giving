The Salvation Army's circa-2000 donation page prominently displayed a Verisign SSL certificate seal — a small graphic that certified the connection was encrypted and the organization's identity was verified. In an era when online fraud was rampant and consumer trust in web transactions was fragile, that seal served as the crucial bridge between a donor's intention and their willingness to type in a credit card number.

This artifact illuminates a largely forgotten challenge of early online fundraising: the trust problem. Charities had to convince donors not only to give, but to give through an unfamiliar and potentially scary medium. The SSL seal, the padlock icon, the reassuring language about encryption — these were not mere decoration. They were essential conversion tools. The Salvation Army, with its century-old brand reputation, was better positioned than most to make the leap, but even they needed the visual reassurance of third-party security validation. The trust infrastructure of online giving — now invisible and taken for granted — had to be built from scratch.
