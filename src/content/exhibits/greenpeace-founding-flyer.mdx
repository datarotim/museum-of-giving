When Greenpeace launched in 1971 with a boat full of activists sailing toward a nuclear test site in Alaska, they simultaneously pioneered a new model of nonprofit fundraising: the spectacle-to-donation pipeline. The dramatic protests generated media coverage; the media coverage drove awareness; and the awareness was converted to donations through aggressive door-to-door canvassing and direct mail campaigns.

Greenpeace essentially industrialized street canvassing, hiring paid canvassers who worked neighborhoods with clipboards, asking for monthly pledges. This face-to-face fundraising model, later adopted by dozens of organizations worldwide, became one of the most reliable donor acquisition channels of the late 20th century. The organization also pioneered the recurring monthly gift as the default ask — a seemingly small shift that dramatically improved donor lifetime value and revenue predictability. Modern sustainer programs trace their DNA directly to Greenpeace's canvassing playbook.
