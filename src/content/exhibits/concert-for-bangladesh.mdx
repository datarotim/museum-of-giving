In July 1971, George Harrison released "Bangla Desh," the first major charity rock single, to raise money and awareness for refugees of the Bangladesh Liberation War. The single preceded his Concert for Bangladesh at Madison Square Garden by weeks — together they represented the invention of celebrity-driven musical fundraising. The concert, featuring Harrison, Ravi Shankar, Bob Dylan, Eric Clapton, and Ringo Starr, raised approximately $243,000 on the day, with the album, film, and single generating millions more for UNICEF relief efforts.

Harrison's single established a template that would transform fundraising over the next two decades: a famous artist lending their name, voice, and audience to a cause. The formula would be replicated and scaled dramatically — by Band Aid's "Do They Know It's Christmas?" in 1984, USA for Africa's "We Are the World" in 1985, and Live Aid's global broadcast that same year. The charity single made giving an act of cultural participation rather than private sacrifice, and it introduced the fan-as-donor archetype that would define broadcast-era fundraising.
