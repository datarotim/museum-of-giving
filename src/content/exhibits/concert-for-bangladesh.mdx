George Harrison's Concert for Bangladesh in 1971 at Madison Square Garden was the prototype for the modern charity concert, raising funds and awareness for refugees of the Bangladesh Liberation War. The event — featuring Harrison, Ravi Shankar, Bob Dylan, Eric Clapton, and Ringo Starr — raised approximately $243,000 on the day, with the album and film generating millions more. By the late 1970s, benefit concerts like No Nukes (1979) had become a recognized format.

These events established an enduring template: a celebrity using their platform and cultural capital to direct attention and money toward a cause. The model would scale dramatically with Live Aid in 1985 and Live 8 in 2005, but the fundamental mechanics were established here. The charity concert also introduced a new donor archetype — the fan who gives not out of guilt or obligation, but out of identification with an artist and their values. It made giving aspirational rather than sacrificial.
