The Indian Ocean tsunami of December 26, 2004, killed over 230,000 people and triggered the largest humanitarian response in history. Online donations poured in at unprecedented rates — Amazon alone processed $15.7 million through a relief link on its homepage. The Red Cross, UNICEF, Oxfam, and dozens of other organizations saw their websites buckle under traffic as donors rushed to give online.

The tsunami response confirmed what 9/11 had suggested: the internet was now a primary channel for crisis philanthropy, not a secondary one. It also introduced new dynamics. Amazon's decision to add a donation link to its homepage pioneered the concept of "giving at the point of commerce" — meeting donors where they already were, rather than requiring them to seek out a charity's website. Corporate platforms became fundraising channels. The disaster also exposed gaps: many organizations couldn't scale their web infrastructure to meet demand, and the donor experience was often frustrating. These failures spurred a wave of investment in nonprofit technology that would transform the sector over the next decade.
