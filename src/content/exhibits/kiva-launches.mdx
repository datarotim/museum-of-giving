Kiva launched in 2005 as the first person-to-person micro-lending platform, allowing individuals to lend as little as $25 to entrepreneurs in developing countries. The model was simple: browse profiles of borrowers — a seamstress in Cambodia, a farmer in Kenya — choose one, lend money, and receive repayment over time. The repaid funds could be re-lent to another borrower, creating a virtuous cycle.

Kiva's innovation was primarily psychological, not financial. By framing the transaction as a loan rather than a donation, Kiva tapped into a different set of motivations: empowerment over pity, partnership over charity, investment over expenditure. The borrower profiles — complete with photos, stories, and business plans — created a sense of connection and agency that traditional appeals rarely achieved. Kiva facilitated over $2 billion in loans with a repayment rate above 96%. The platform demonstrated that the mechanics of giving matter as much as the ask — and that reframing the relationship between giver and receiver could unlock entirely new pools of generosity.
