In 1996, the American Red Cross became one of the first major charities to accept donations through its website. The page was rudimentary — a basic HTML form asking for credit card details, transmitted with early SSL encryption. There was no recurring gift option, no suggested amounts, no impact statement. Just fields, a submit button, and a leap of faith from both the organization and the donor.

This was a genuinely uncertain moment. Consumer trust in online transactions barely existed; Amazon was a year old, and most people had never bought anything on the internet, let alone given money away through it. The Red Cross wagered that its brand trust, built over a century of service, could bridge the credibility gap of a new medium. That wager paid off. Within a few years, online donations went from novelty to necessity, and the basic architecture of the web donation form — amount, payment details, submit — was established in these earliest experiments.
