Liberty Bond advertisements ran in nearly every American newspaper during 1917–1918, urging citizens to lend their savings to the war effort. The ads blended patriotic imagery with financial language — "Your money fights" — and introduced installment giving, allowing even modest earners to participate. Celebrities from Charlie Chaplin to Douglas Fairbanks headlined rallies; Boy Scouts sold bonds door-to-door.

The Liberty Bond campaigns raised over $21 billion (roughly $400 billion in today's dollars) and, more importantly, established a durable idea: that ordinary people could participate in great causes through financial contribution. The fundraising infrastructure built for these bonds — direct appeals, social pressure, celebrity endorsement, payroll deduction — would be borrowed by every major charity drive for the next century.
