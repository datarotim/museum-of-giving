On January 28, 1985, 46 of the biggest names in American music gathered at A&M Studios in Hollywood to record "We Are the World." Written by Michael Jackson and Lionel Richie and produced by Quincy Jones, the single was a direct response to the Ethiopian famine and a conscious American counterpart to Band Aid's British "Do They Know It's Christmas?" The roster read like a hall of fame: Stevie Wonder, Bruce Springsteen, Tina Turner, Bob Dylan, Ray Charles, Diana Ross, and dozens more. The single sold over 20 million copies and raised more than $63 million for humanitarian aid in Africa.

"We Are the World" was more than a hit record — it was a proof of concept. It demonstrated that the charity single could operate at industrial scale, generating tens of millions of dollars through a consumer product people actually wanted. The song dominated radio, MTV, and retail simultaneously, reaching donors who would never respond to a direct mail appeal or telethon. It also established a playbook for celebrity-cause alignment that nonprofits would mine for decades: assemble enough star power and the cause almost markets itself. The single's legacy is complicated — critics questioned whether feel-good anthems oversimplified African crises — but its fundraising impact was undeniable and its influence on broadcast-era giving permanent.
