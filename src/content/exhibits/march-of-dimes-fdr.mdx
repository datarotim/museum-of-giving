When Franklin D. Roosevelt, himself a polio survivor, helped establish what would become the March of Dimes, the organization pioneered a radical fundraising thesis: ask everyone for a little instead of asking the wealthy for a lot. Eddie Cantor's 1938 radio appeal coined the name "March of Dimes," urging listeners to mail dimes directly to the White House. The response overwhelmed the mail room — 2.6 million dimes arrived in the first month alone.

This was arguably the first mass micro-donation campaign in history. It proved that emotional connection to a cause, combined with a trivially low barrier to entry, could generate staggering sums. The model prefigured everything from telethons to text-to-give to crowdfunding. The dime on the dollar became a symbol of something larger: the democratization of generosity itself.
