The YMCA's "For Your Boy" poster was part of the November 1918 United War Work Campaign and represented the organization's massive wartime welfare operation. The YMCA ran canteens, recreation huts, libraries, and entertainment programs for American troops across France and at military camps throughout the United States. At its peak, the YMCA operated over 4,000 facilities and employed more than 25,000 workers overseas. The emotional core of the appeal was simple and devastating: your son is far from home, and your donation keeps him safe, sane, and connected to the life he left behind.

The "For Your Boy" framing was a masterpiece of donor psychology. Rather than asking Americans to support an abstract institution, the YMCA made each donor's contribution personal — a direct line from parent to child. This technique of collapsing the emotional distance between donor and beneficiary became one of the most durable strategies in fundraising history. The YMCA's wartime fundraising also demonstrated the power of service-based giving: donors were not funding the organization itself but the tangible services it delivered. Hot chocolate, writing paper, a place to rest — these concrete deliverables gave donors a mental picture of impact, foreshadowing the "your $25 provides..." framing that would dominate nonprofit direct mail for the next century.
