In 2007, the Causes application launched on Facebook, allowing users to create and join cause pages, recruit friends, and solicit donations — all within the social network. It was one of the first attempts to harness social media for charitable fundraising. Millions of users added Causes to their profiles, broadcasting their philanthropic affiliations to their networks.

The fundraising results were initially modest — most causes raised small sums — but the social signaling was significant. For the first time, charitable giving became a visible, shareable social act in a digital space. Users didn't just give; they told their friends they gave. This public declaration of values would prove to be the key insight of social fundraising: the act of sharing a cause was as valuable as the donation itself, because it triggered network effects. Each share was an implicit endorsement to hundreds of friends. The mechanics were primitive, but Facebook Causes demonstrated the thesis that would underpin the next decade of social fundraising: giving is inherently social, and the best fundraising tools make it easy to give publicly.
