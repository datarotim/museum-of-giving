World Relief, the humanitarian arm of the National Association of Evangelicals, developed direct mail appeals that focused on specific communities and programs rather than abstract global needs. This appeal for Karungu, Kenya highlighted the organization's work in a particular community, giving donors a geographic and human anchor for their giving. The letter format followed direct mail best practices: a personal salutation, a story from the field, a clear ask, and a response mechanism.

The specificity of the Karungu appeal reflects a lesson that direct mail practitioners learned through decades of testing: donors respond to the particular, not the general. "Help children in Kenya" underperforms "help children in Karungu, a community on the shores of Lake Victoria." This principle — sometimes called the "identifiable victim effect" — became a cornerstone of direct mail copywriting. By naming a place, describing its challenges, and connecting the donor's gift to outcomes in that specific community, World Relief's appeal transformed an international aid organization into something that felt personal and local.
