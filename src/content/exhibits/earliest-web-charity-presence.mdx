By 1995, the first nonprofit organizations had established websites on the World Wide Web. The American Red Cross, United Way, and a handful of other large charities created simple informational pages — mission statements, program descriptions, mailing addresses for donations. There were no online donation forms; the "call to action" was typically a phone number or a postal address. The pages loaded slowly over dial-up connections and were visited by a tiny fraction of the public.

Yet these modest HTML pages marked a fundamental inflection point. For the first time, a charitable organization could maintain a permanent, globally accessible, always-on presence at near-zero marginal cost. A donor could learn about a cause at 2 AM on a Tuesday without waiting for a mailer or a broadcast. The information asymmetry between charities and donors began to dissolve. Within a decade, the "donate now" button would transform these brochure-ware sites into transaction engines. But in 1995, simply being on the web was the innovation.
