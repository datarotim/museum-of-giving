By 2001, World Vision had brought its signature child sponsorship model to the web, featuring a prominent "Sponsor a Child Now" button. The organization, which had built its brand through television appeals and direct mail campaigns featuring photos and stories of individual children, recognized early that the internet could make the sponsorship experience more immediate and personal. Online, a donor could browse profiles, select a child, and begin a recurring monthly commitment — all without waiting for a mailer or calling a phone number.

World Vision's move online is significant because child sponsorship was one of the first fundraising models purpose-built for recurring giving, and the web made recurring commitments dramatically easier to set up and maintain. The model also translated naturally to digital because it was inherently visual and story-driven: a photo, a name, a country, a need. The web could deliver this emotional specificity at scale in ways that even television could not, and without the per-contact cost of direct mail. World Vision's early adoption of online sponsorship helped establish the template for digital recurring giving programs that would become central to nonprofit revenue strategy in the decades that followed.
