In 1948, the United Jewish Appeal launched an urgent magazine advertising campaign tied to two converging crises: the resettlement of hundreds of thousands of Holocaust survivors still displaced across Europe, and the founding and defense of the new State of Israel. The ads combined stark imagery with moral urgency, calling on American Jews and sympathetic allies to give at unprecedented levels. The campaign's emotional power was rooted in recent history — the full horror of the Holocaust was still emerging, and the need was undeniable.

The UJA campaign shattered existing fundraising records. In the late 1940s, the organization raised over $200 million annually — an extraordinary sum equivalent to billions in today's dollars. The campaign demonstrated that cause urgency, historical moment, and community identity could combine to unlock giving at a scale previously unimaginable. It also established a model for diaspora fundraising that influenced international humanitarian appeals for decades. The UJA's success proved that print advertising, when matched to the right moment, could mobilize an entire community.
