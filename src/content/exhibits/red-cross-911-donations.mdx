On September 11, 2001, the American Red Cross received approximately 41,000 online donations within 24 hours — a volume that overwhelmed their servers and shattered all existing records for internet-based giving. The total online response in the weeks following the attacks reached tens of millions of dollars, demonstrating for the first time that the internet could function as a primary channel for crisis philanthropy at scale.

9/11 was the inflection point for online giving. Before that day, web donations were a supplement to mail and phone — a modest and somewhat experimental channel. After it, every major charity began investing seriously in online fundraising infrastructure. The crisis also revealed the internet's unique advantage in disaster response: immediacy. Donors could act on their emotions in the moment of impact, without waiting for a mailer or finding a phone. The gap between impulse and action collapsed to the time it takes to fill out a form. This speed would prove even more consequential in future disasters — the 2004 tsunami, Hurricane Katrina, the Haiti earthquake — each of which set new records for online giving.
