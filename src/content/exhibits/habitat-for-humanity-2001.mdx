By 2001, Habitat for Humanity had established a web presence that reflected its distinctive model: a charity built as much on volunteer labor as on financial donations. The site served as a hub for connecting supporters with local affiliate chapters, finding upcoming build projects, and making online donations. For an organization whose brand was built on the visceral experience of building a house alongside the family who would live in it, the challenge was translating that tangible, physical mission into a digital medium.

Habitat's early website illustrates a broader challenge that many charities faced in the early web era. Organizations with strong experiential or volunteer-driven models had to figure out how to use the internet not just to collect money, but to recruit and coordinate human participation. The web became a logistics tool as much as a fundraising tool — connecting willing hands with local needs at a scale that phone trees and bulletin boards never could. Habitat's approach presaged the modern nonprofit website as a multi-purpose platform: part donation page, part volunteer portal, part storytelling engine.
