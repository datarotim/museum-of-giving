Beginning in 1950 and reaching peak cultural saturation by the early 1960s, UNICEF's orange Trick-or-Treat boxes became a fixture of American Halloween. Children carried the small cardboard boxes alongside their candy bags, collecting coins from neighbors for UNICEF. At its height, the program raised tens of millions of dollars annually and involved millions of young volunteers.

The genius was multi-layered. UNICEF recruited an unpaid, enthusiastic salesforce (children). It leveraged an existing cultural ritual (trick-or-treating) rather than competing for attention. It introduced children to the habit of giving at the earliest possible age. And it turned every household doorstep into a point of solicitation. The program was, in retrospect, a proto-peer-to-peer campaign — decades before the concept had a name in fundraising circles.
