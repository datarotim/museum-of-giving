The Salvation Army's "Oh Boy! That's the Girl!" poster celebrated the famous Doughnut Lassies — women volunteers who served American troops in France by frying doughnuts and providing comfort near the front lines. Beginning in 1917, Salvation Army women worked in makeshift huts just behind the trenches, often under dangerous conditions, making doughnuts in steel helmets when proper equipment was unavailable. The doughnuts became legendary among soldiers, and the Lassies earned a reputation for going closer to the front than any other welfare organization. This poster, part of the 1918 United War Work Campaign, asked Americans to fund the continuation of their service.

The poster's genius lay in its personalization. Rather than asking donors to support the Salvation Army as an institution, it asked them to "keep her on the job" — a specific, identifiable woman doing specific, tangible work. This was an early and powerful example of what modern fundraisers call the "identifiable victim effect": donors give more when they can picture a single individual rather than a statistical mass. The Doughnut Lassies also transformed the Salvation Army's public image in America. Before the war, the organization was primarily associated with street-corner evangelism and urban poverty work. After the war, it was one of the most trusted and beloved charities in the country — a reputation built almost entirely on the wartime fundraising campaign and the powerful symbol of a woman frying doughnuts under fire.
