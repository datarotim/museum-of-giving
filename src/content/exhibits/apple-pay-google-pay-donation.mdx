By 2021, leading nonprofit donation platforms had integrated Apple Pay and Google Pay as primary payment options, reducing the donation process to a single tap or biometric confirmation. No typing credit card numbers, no creating accounts, no filling out address fields. A donor could see an appeal on social media, tap through to a donation form, and complete the gift in under ten seconds — authenticated by their face or fingerprint.

The integration of mobile wallet payments into fundraising represented the logical endpoint of decades of friction reduction. From mailing a check (days), to calling a phone number (minutes), to filling out a web form (minutes), to tapping a payment button (seconds) — each generation of giving technology compressed the gap between intention and action. Apple Pay and Google Pay also solved the mobile conversion problem that had plagued nonprofits: donation forms designed for desktop converted poorly on phones because typing payment details on a small screen was tedious. Mobile wallets bypassed the keyboard entirely. Organizations that added Apple Pay saw mobile conversion rates increase by 30-50%, unlocking revenue that was previously lost to form abandonment.
