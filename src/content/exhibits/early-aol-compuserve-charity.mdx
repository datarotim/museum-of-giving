In the early 1990s, before the World Wide Web reached mainstream adoption, online services like AOL and CompuServe hosted some of the first digital discussions about charitable giving. Nonprofit organizations began establishing presences in these walled-garden communities — posting in forums, creating keyword-accessible pages, and tentatively exploring whether this new medium could be used to solicit donations.

These early digital outposts were crude by any standard: text-only, buried in menu hierarchies, accessible only to the small population of modem-equipped early adopters. But they represented something genuinely new — the first time a charity could communicate with potential donors without printing, postage, or broadcast airtime. The marginal cost of reaching one more person approached zero for the first time in fundraising history. It would take years for the technology and trust to catch up to the potential, but the seed of online giving was planted here, in the amber glow of 14.4kbps dial-up connections.
