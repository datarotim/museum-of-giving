In the summer of 2014, the ALS Ice Bucket Challenge swept across social media with astonishing velocity. The mechanic was simple: dump a bucket of ice water on your head, post the video, nominate three friends to do the same within 24 hours, and donate to ALS research. The challenge was taken up by celebrities, politicians, athletes, and millions of ordinary people. The ALS Association received $115 million in donations over six weeks — compared to $2.8 million in the same period the previous year.

The Ice Bucket Challenge was the most successful viral fundraising campaign in history, and it defied nearly every convention of nonprofit fundraising. There was no carefully crafted appeal letter, no celebrity spokesperson chosen by a marketing team, no media buy. The campaign was organic, participatory, and fun. It succeeded because it gave people a way to perform generosity publicly, to be part of a cultural moment, and to connect with friends through a shared experience. The donation was almost secondary to the social ritual. The challenge also funded real science — the donations directly contributed to the discovery of the NEK1 gene associated with ALS. It proved that when fundraising becomes entertainment, the results can be extraordinary.
