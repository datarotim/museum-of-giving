Harrison Fisher's 1918 poster for the American Red Cross transformed charitable giving from a private act into a public duty. The image of a nurse beckoning beneath the words "I Summon You to Comradeship" was printed in the millions and plastered across every post office, train station, and shopfront in the country. It was among the first campaigns to apply commercial advertising techniques — emotional imagery, a clear call to action, mass distribution — to the cause of philanthropy.

The poster arrived at a turning point: the Great War demanded resources on a scale no charity had ever attempted. The Red Cross membership drive that accompanied it grew the organization from 17,000 to over 20 million members in a single year. The visual grammar it established — the heroic figure, the direct appeal, the institutional brand — became the template for a century of nonprofit communications to come.
