St. Jude Children's Research Hospital, founded by Danny Thomas in 1962, built one of the most formidable direct mail fundraising programs in American philanthropy. By the 1970s, their appeal letters had been refined into a science: a compelling patient story, a photograph of a child, a clear explanation of the hospital's no-family-pays model, and a reply envelope with suggested gift amounts.

The program was notable for its relentless testing and optimization. St. Jude tested everything — envelope color, letter length, photo placement, ask strings, postscripts. They discovered that handwritten-looking fonts outperformed typed ones, that odd dollar amounts ($17) sometimes outperformed round ones ($20), and that a P.S. at the bottom of the letter was the second-most-read element after the headline. This disciplined, data-driven approach to direct mail turned St. Jude into one of the largest and best-funded charities in the world, raising billions through the mail.
