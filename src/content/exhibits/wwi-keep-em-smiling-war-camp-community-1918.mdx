War Camp Community Service was established in 1917 under the auspices of the Playground and Recreation Association of America to organize community-based support for soldiers stationed at nearby military camps. The organization coordinated local volunteers to provide entertainment, social events, home hospitality, and recreational facilities for servicemen who were far from their own families and communities. Its "Keep 'Em Smiling" campaign was part of the broader United War Work Campaign of 1918, which united seven welfare organizations in a single national fundraising drive.

The significance of War Camp Community Service in fundraising history lies in its community organizing model. Rather than asking individuals to mail checks to a distant national headquarters, the organization mobilized entire towns to contribute time, resources, and money locally for a cause with national significance. This hyperlocal approach to fundraising — where the donor could see the beneficiary walking down their own Main Street — created an immediacy of impact that national campaigns struggled to match. The model influenced the development of Community Chest campaigns in the 1920s and demonstrated that effective fundraising could flow from community identity and local pride as much as from patriotic duty or emotional appeals.
