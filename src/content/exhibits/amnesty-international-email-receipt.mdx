This May 1998 capture of Amnesty International's website shows the organization using the web to mark the 50th anniversary of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. The prominent "Sign Up for Human Rights" campaign invited visitors to make a personal commitment — an early form of digital activism that predated the petition platforms and social sharing tools that would follow.

Amnesty's 1998 web presence is notable for how early it demonstrated the internet's potential as an advocacy tool, not just an information repository. While most nonprofit websites of the era were static brochures, Amnesty was already using the web to mobilize supporters around specific campaigns and actions. The site's navigation included "Act Now," "Campaigns," and "Interactive" sections — language that signaled a participatory model fundamentally different from the passive consumption of printed materials. This approach, combining awareness with a call to action in a single digital session, would become the template for advocacy-driven fundraising in the decades that followed.
