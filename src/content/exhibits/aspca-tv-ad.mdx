The ASPCA's televised appeals, which began in the late 1980s and reached their cultural apex with the Sarah McLachlan "Angel" campaign in the 2000s, represent the pinnacle of emotional direct-response television fundraising. The formula was devastatingly simple: close-up footage of suffering animals, a mournful soundtrack, a compassionate narrator, and a phone number or URL displayed continuously.

These ads became a cultural phenomenon — parodied, mocked, and yet extraordinarily effective. The McLachlan spots alone raised over $30 million in their first two years and brought in hundreds of thousands of new donors. The ASPCA proved that emotional intensity, sustained over a long-form spot, could override the viewer's natural resistance to solicitation. The ads also demonstrated a uncomfortable truth about fundraising: the techniques that draw the most criticism often raise the most money. They remain a case study in the tension between effectiveness and dignity in nonprofit communications.
