In the mid-1960s, Christian Children's Fund ran full-page magazine advertisements featuring photographs of individual children alongside emotional copy and a tear-out donation coupon. The format was deceptively simple: a child's face, a few paragraphs describing her circumstances, and a dotted-line form at the bottom where readers could commit to a monthly sponsorship. These ads appeared in mass-circulation magazines like Life, Look, and Reader's Digest, reaching millions of American households.

CCF's magazine ads established the template that would later dominate television fundraising. The "one child, one donor" sponsorship model — complete with promised letters and progress photos — created a sense of personal connection that generic charity appeals couldn't match. When Sally Struthers brought the same formula to TV screens in the 1980s, she was amplifying a playbook that CCF had already perfected in print two decades earlier. The magazine coupon was the original "call to action," and the sponsor-a-child model it popularized remains one of the most successful fundraising frameworks in history.
