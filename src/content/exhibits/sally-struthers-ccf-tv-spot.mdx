In the early 1980s, Christian Children's Fund (later ChildFund International) deployed actress Sally Struthers as the face of their televised child sponsorship appeals. The long-form commercials — often two minutes or more — showed Struthers walking through impoverished communities, speaking directly to camera, and asking viewers to sponsor a child "for just pennies a day." A toll-free number stayed on screen throughout.

These spots perfected the emotional direct-response formula for television. They introduced the "child sponsorship" model to a mass audience: one donor, one child, a monthly commitment, regular letters and photos. The personal connection — "this is Maria, she is seven years old" — made an abstract problem feel solvable. The format was enormously effective and widely imitated, though it later drew criticism for its portrayal of developing nations. Love it or critique it, this style of advertising defined how an entire generation understood international charity.
