By 1997, the American Red Cross had refined its online donation page to include features that seem obvious in retrospect but were genuine innovations at the time: pre-set donation amounts ($25, $50, $100), a dropdown to designate which program the gift should support, and a confirmation page that acknowledged the transaction. The form also began collecting email addresses — planting the seed for the digital donor communication that would follow.

These incremental improvements to the donation form UX established patterns that persist to this day. The suggested amount array — anchoring donors to specific numbers rather than leaving an open field — was a behavioral design choice with enormous revenue implications. Research would later show that suggested amounts can increase average gift size by 15-30%. The designation option gave donors a sense of control and specificity. Each element of the modern donation form can be traced to these early iterative experiments in the late 1990s.
