In the depths of the Great Depression, the Salvation Army's Christmas appeal posters depicted scenes of warmth and rescue — families fed, children clothed, hope restored. The iconic red kettles and bell ringers had already been fixtures since the 1890s, but the Depression-era print campaigns elevated seasonal giving into a national ritual. The messaging was simple and direct: your gift means a hot meal for someone who has nothing.

The Salvation Army effectively invented the concept of the year-end giving season. By anchoring charitable appeals to Christmas — a time of emotional openness and social obligation — they created a fundraising calendar that the entire nonprofit sector still follows. To this day, roughly one-third of all annual charitable giving in the United States occurs in December, a pattern that traces directly back to campaigns like this one.
