Charity Navigator, launched in 2001 by Pat and Marion Dugan, was the first major website dedicated to rating charitable organizations based on their financial health and accountability. By 2005, it had become the go-to resource for donors who wanted to verify that their money was being used effectively. The site assigned star ratings based on publicly available IRS filings, making nonprofit financial data accessible to ordinary donors for the first time.

The impact on the sector was profound and sometimes controversial. Charity Navigator's emphasis on overhead ratios — the percentage of spending on programs versus administration and fundraising — shaped donor expectations for years, pressuring nonprofits to minimize overhead even when investment in infrastructure might have improved outcomes. The site also demonstrated a fundamental truth about online giving: the web didn't just create new ways to donate, it created new ways to evaluate. Donors who once relied on brand recognition or a compelling mailer could now research an organization's finances in minutes. Transparency became a competitive advantage, and charities that couldn't withstand scrutiny found it harder to raise money in the digital age.
