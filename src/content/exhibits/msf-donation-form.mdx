The Doctors Without Borders donation form distills decades of fundraising optimization into a clean, focused interface. The one-time and monthly toggle sits prominently at the top, with preset amounts of $15, $30, $50, and $100 that reflect careful testing of donor price sensitivity. The lowest amount — $15 — is low enough to minimize hesitation, while the $100 option anchors the scale upward, making the middle options feel reasonable by comparison. This anchoring technique, borrowed from behavioral economics, is now standard practice but MSF's implementation is particularly refined.

For an organization operating in active conflict zones and disaster areas, the donation form must bridge the gap between emotional urgency and sustained commitment. MSF's form achieves this by making monthly giving the path of least resistance — the toggle is simple, the amounts are modest, and the implicit message is that ongoing crises require ongoing support. The form represents the evolution from the early days of online humanitarian appeals, when organizations like Red Cross first experimented with web donations after 9/11, to today's optimized conversion engines. Every pixel has been tested, every default has been chosen deliberately, and the result is a form that converts compassion into reliable, predictable revenue.
