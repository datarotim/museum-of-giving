By the mid-1970s, United Way had perfected a fundraising model unlike anything else in American philanthropy: the workplace campaign. Through partnerships with major employers, United Way ran annual drives where employees could pledge a portion of their paycheck to a pooled community fund. Magazine advertisements reinforced the message nationally, positioning United Way as the single most efficient way to support dozens of local charities with one gift. The ads emphasized community impact and the simplicity of payroll giving.

The federated campaign model was a structural innovation as much as a marketing one. Instead of competing with hundreds of individual charities for donor attention, United Way aggregated demand — one ask, one pledge, distributed across many organizations. At its peak, United Way raised over $5 billion annually, making it the largest fundraising organization in the United States. The model also introduced millions of Americans to charitable giving through the low-friction mechanism of payroll deduction, a concept that anticipated the recurring digital donations of the internet era by decades.
