When COVID-19 shut down the world in March 2020, nonprofits that had been slowly migrating to digital fundraising were forced to complete the transition overnight. In-person events were cancelled, direct mail production was disrupted, and face-to-face fundraising ceased entirely. Every organization pivoted to online-only appeals, and the results were remarkable: online giving grew 21% in 2020, with some organizations seeing 50-100% increases in digital donations.

The pandemic compressed a decade of digital transformation into months. Organizations that had resisted online fundraising had no choice but to adopt it. Virtual galas, livestream fundraisers, and digital peer-to-peer campaigns replaced their physical counterparts. Email and social media became primary communication channels by necessity. The crisis also revealed deep disparities in digital readiness: organizations with mature online programs thrived, while those without scrambled to build capabilities from scratch. COVID didn't create the digital fundraising era — it made it mandatory. The sector that emerged on the other side was fundamentally, irreversibly more digital than the one that entered it.
