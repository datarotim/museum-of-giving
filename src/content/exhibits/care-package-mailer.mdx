The CARE Package, originally created to send food to post-war Europe, evolved in the 1950s into one of the most effective direct mail fundraising programs in history. The mailers were straightforward: a photo of a family, a description of what the package contained, and a specific dollar amount tied to a specific outcome. "Send a CARE Package for $10" was a masterclass in donor psychology before the field had a name.

What CARE pioneered was the tangible ask — connecting a donation amount to a concrete deliverable. This seemingly simple innovation solved one of philanthropy's persistent problems: the abstraction of impact. Donors no longer wondered where their money went. They could picture the package, imagine the recipient, and feel the transaction was complete. Every "fund a meal for $5" or "buy a mosquito net for $2" appeal in modern fundraising descends from CARE's direct mail playbook.
