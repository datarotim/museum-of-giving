GoFundMe, launched in 2010 and reaching critical mass by 2013, created the world's largest personal fundraising platform. Unlike charity-focused platforms, GoFundMe allowed anyone to raise money for any purpose — medical bills, disaster recovery, memorial funds, education expenses, community projects. The platform's social integration made sharing campaigns frictionless, and its lack of gatekeeping (no charitable registration required) opened fundraising to individuals and causes that fell outside the traditional nonprofit framework.

GoFundMe has facilitated over $30 billion in donations, making it one of the largest fundraising platforms in history. Its impact is complex. On one hand, it democratized giving in unprecedented ways, enabling communities to rally around individuals in crisis with extraordinary speed. On the other, the prevalence of medical fundraising campaigns — one-third of all GoFundMe campaigns are health-related — became an uncomfortable mirror for systemic failures in healthcare and social services. GoFundMe revealed that when institutions fail, individuals will build their own safety nets, one $25 donation at a time. Whether that represents empowerment or tragedy depends on your perspective.
