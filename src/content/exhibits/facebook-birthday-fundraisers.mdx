In 2015, Facebook began rolling out birthday fundraisers, allowing users to create a fundraising campaign for a nonprofit in lieu of birthday gifts. The feature was seamlessly integrated into the birthday notification flow — when Facebook reminded your friends about your birthday, it also prompted them to donate. The friction was minimal: a few taps, payment processed through Facebook, and a public feed post celebrating the donation.

The birthday fundraiser succeeded because it piggybacked on an existing social behavior with near-universal participation. Everyone has a birthday; everyone's friends see the notification. By inserting a giving prompt into this natural social touchpoint, Facebook converted a passive interaction (posting "happy birthday!") into an active one (donating $20). The feature generated over $7 billion in donations by 2023, much of it from people who had never made an online charitable donation before. It demonstrated a principle that the best fundraising products don't ask people to change their behavior — they embed giving into behavior that already exists.
