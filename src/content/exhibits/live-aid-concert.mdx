On July 13, 1985, Live Aid broadcast simultaneously from Wembley Stadium in London and JFK Stadium in Philadelphia to an estimated 1.9 billion viewers across 150 countries. Organized by Bob Geldof and Midge Ure in response to the Ethiopian famine, it featured Queen, U2, David Bowie, Elton John, and dozens more. The event raised approximately $127 million.

Live Aid was the first truly global fundraising event — a charity concert scaled to planetary proportions via satellite television. Geldof's on-air outbursts ("Give us your money!") dispensed with the polite conventions of philanthropic asking and introduced an urgency that resonated with a generation. The event demonstrated that media technology could compress the distance between a crisis and a donor's wallet to nearly zero. It also revealed the tensions that would define the next four decades of charity: the gap between awareness and impact, the complexity of aid delivery, and the question of whether spectacle helps or obscures the causes it claims to serve.
