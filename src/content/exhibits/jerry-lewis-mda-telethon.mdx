Jerry Lewis first hosted the Muscular Dystrophy Association telethon in 1966, beginning a tradition that would run for 45 consecutive years and raise over $2.6 billion. The Labor Day broadcast became an American institution — a 21-hour marathon of comedy, music, celebrity appearances, and emotional appeals that reliably delivered both ratings and revenue.

Lewis perfected the telethon formula: the host as emotional anchor, the "Jerry's Kids" segments that personalized the disease, the corporate sponsors matching gifts in real time, the tote board climbing toward an ever-larger goal. He understood that sustained emotional engagement over hours would produce donations that a 30-second spot never could. The format also demonstrated the power of recurring annual events — building donor habits, brand association, and institutional loyalty over decades. It was appointment philanthropy.
