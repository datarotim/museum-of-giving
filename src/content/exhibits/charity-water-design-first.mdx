When Scott Harrison founded charity: water in 2006, he brought something the nonprofit sector had rarely seen: a brand-first, design-first approach to charitable giving. The website was clean, modern, and visually stunning — more Apple than ASPCA. The messaging was radical in its simplicity: 100% of public donations go directly to water projects, with operating costs covered separately by private donors. Every completed project was marked with GPS coordinates on a public map.

charity: water challenged the prevailing nonprofit aesthetic of earnest amateurism. It demonstrated that the donor experience — visual design, brand identity, storytelling, transparency — was itself a fundraising tool. The organization raised over $100 million in its first seven years, attracting a younger, design-conscious donor demographic that traditional charities struggled to reach. It also pioneered the "birthday campaign" model, encouraging supporters to forgo gifts and ask friends to donate instead. charity: water proved that a nonprofit could compete for attention and dollars not through guilt, but through aspiration — by making donors feel they were joining something beautiful.
