First Charity Telethon — Cerebral Palsy

United Cerebral Palsy · 1952 · US
Broadcast

Television turned fundraising into live entertainment. The telethon proved that if you could hold an audience's attention long enough, guilt and goodwill would do the rest.

In 1952, United Cerebral Palsy staged one of the first major charity telethons, broadcasting for hours on end and asking viewers to phone in pledges. The format was simple but revolutionary: keep the cameras rolling, parade a succession of entertainers and emotional stories across the screen, and flash the phone number constantly. The tote board — a running tally of donations — turned giving into a spectator sport.

The telethon merged entertainment, emotion, and immediacy in a way print never could. Viewers watched in real time as the total climbed, creating social proof and competitive urgency. The format would be refined over the next four decades by Jerry Lewis, Comic Relief, and countless local telethons, but the core innovation was established here: broadcasting transformed fundraising from a private transaction into a shared cultural event.
