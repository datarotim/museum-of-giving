About the Museum

In 1998, someone donated to Amnesty International online. The receipt that came back looked almost identical to an e-commerce order confirmation. Item: one human rights donation. Qty: 1. It was unremarkable and, for that exact reason, remarkable.

That moment captured something we keep forgetting: the way nonprofits ask for money is a mirror of how the world communicates. From hand-painted war bond posters to AI-optimized donation forms, every era's technology reshapes the act of giving.

The Museum of Giving exists to document this evolution. Not the history of philanthropy itself — there are books for that — but the artifacts. The actual posters, letters, web pages, text messages, and social posts through which organizations have asked ordinary people to help.

We believe these artifacts deserve to be preserved. Many are already gone. Early donation pages from the 1990s have vanished from the internet. Direct mail letters from the 1960s sit in basements, fading. Telethon footage is locked in broadcast archives. Each one tells a story about how we learned to give.
